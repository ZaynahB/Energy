Specific Heat Capacity

Today we did a Gizmo that looked at specific heat capacity. Specific heat capacity is the amount of energy that is needed to increase the temperature of 1kg of a particular substance by 1°C. Specific heat capacity is measured in [J / kg °C].

Using the Gizmo, we used a calorimeter to find an object’s specific heat capacity. A calorimeter measures heat. A calorimeter is an insulated container filled with a liquid, usually water. When a hot object is placed in the calorimeter, heat energy is transferred from the object to the water and the water heats up.

Using this, we would know the final temperature of the water and the object, as well as the change in temperature for each substance.

The equation for determining heat energy is q = mc∆t. Q is the heat energy transferred (measured in J), m is the mass (measured in kg), c is the specific heat capacity (measured in J/kg°C) and ∆t is the change in temperature (measured in °C).

One question I have after learning this is what happens when the material changes state? Would the equation or concepts be different?

One thing that really intrigues me is that water has a very high specific heat capacity. This means that it is very hard to change the temperature of water. Water has a specific heat capacity of 4182 J/kg°C. A Calorie is the amount of energy that it takes to raise one gram of water by one °C. This is different from the kind of calorie that we talk about in food. That is equivalent to 1000 Calories.

Water (comprised of two hydrogen atoms and one oxygen atom) has a higher specific heat than many other common substances. This is because water is electronegative. An electronegative atom is more likely to draw electrons to itself, because one side of the atom will have a partially positive charge and other will have a partially negative charge. The opposite charged sides are naturally drawn to one another, forming a weaker hydrogen bond. This is why water is able to flow past itself but also bond together; it is constantly forming and breaking these bonds.
