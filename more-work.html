What is Work?

Tuesday, November 26, 2019

26 Nov.

By: Zaynah Bhanji
More Work Scenarios

In the last post, I had a lot of questions about work and thought about a few other scenarios in which the formula W = FΔd would not necessarily work.

The first scenario is when force isn’t in the same direction as the motion of the object. It turns out, there is a formula for that! In this case:

W = F⋅Δd⋅cosθ

The above image is the scenario where this formula would be applicable.

We also talked about kinetic energy and gravitational potential energy, 2 different ways in which energy is stored.

Kinetic energy is the energy an object has because of its motion. If you want to accelerate an object, you need to apply a force. Applying a force requires us to do work in this scenario, and after work has been done, energy has been transferred to the object. The energy transferred is called kinetic energy, and to determine this, we need to look at the mass and speed of the object.

To determine kinetic energy, we use this formula where m is the mass and v is the velocity:

Kinetic Energy = 1/2⋅m⋅v ^2

Gravitational potential energy is energy an object possesses because of its position in a gravitational field. The most common use of this is for an object near the surface of the earth where the gravitational acceleration is about 9.8 m/s^2.

To determine gravitational potential energy, we use this formula where m is mass, g is gravity, and h is height of the object above the ground:

Gravitational Potential Energy = m⋅g⋅h

This is getting very granular into each of these concepts, however there is an underlaying concept that is the most important. When calculating the total energy transfer from moment A to moment B, this is the most important concept:

Total Energy at Moment A = Total Energy at Moment B

Using this concept as well as an LOL diagram, you are able to visualize the whole energy system and then you can get more granular into figuring out each side of the equation.

I have a few questions regarding gravitational potential energy that I did some further research on. Firstly, what happens if the gravitational field is not uniform? You would not be able to calculate the gravitational potential energy this way? And in what scenario would this even be the case?

This would be the case in the scenario that the problem involves large distances. We can use Newton’s law of gravitation, which is:

F = (G⋅m1⋅m2) / r^2

This means that the force (F) between two masses (m1 and m2) decreases with the separation distance (r^2). It turns out that we do need calculus to figure out a formula for this scenario, however, I can try to explain it simply. As the distance (r) becomes larger and larger, the gravitational force rapidly goes towards zero.

When you are close to a planet, you are bound to the planet by gravity and need a lot of energy to escape. You can only escape when r = infinity. However, because of the inverse square relationship we can reach an asymptote where gravitational potential energy becomes very close to zero.

So, the formula then becomes:

Gravitational Potential Energy(r) = –(G⋅m1⋅m2) / r^2

This concept itself is very intriguing and interesting, and I hope we talk more about it in grade 12 :)

One thing I find very intriguing about kinetic energy is that it depends on the velocity squared. This means that if you double the velocity of an object, its kinetic energy quadruples. In addition, kinetic energy is not a vector, meaning that if you throw a ball to the right or to the left, it will have the same kinetic energy.
