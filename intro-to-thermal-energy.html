Internal Energy, Temperature & Heat

Today we started a new part of the Energy unit, looking at thermal energy. We started today by looking at internal energy, temperature and heat.

Internal energy is the sum of kinetic energies and potential energies of the particles in a substance. The internal energy can be changed by heating or taking heat from the object as well as doing work on the object or having the object do work. To determine the kinetic energy, we need to look at the movement of atoms and molecules. Atoms and molecules are in constant motion, and the higher the degree of motion, the higher the kinetic energy. To determine the potential energy, we can imagine the particles as a spring. When the spring is pushed or pulled together, energy is stored. Just like this, there is potential energy between the particles in a substance.

Temperature is a measure of the average random kinetic energy of the particles in a substance. The faster the average speed of the particles, the hotter the temperature. There are 3 scales that we use to measure temperature, Kelvin (°K), Celsius (°C), and Fahrenheit (°F). In the Celsius scale, the value of 0°C is assigned to the freezing point of water and the value of 100°C is assigned to the boiling point of water. In the Kelvin scale, 0 is the lowest possible temperature, meaning that there are no negative values on the Kelvin scale. The total °K is equal to the total °C + 273. This means that -273°C is the lowest value on the °C scale.

Heat means the same thing as thermal energy. Thermal energy is energy that is transferred from one body to another as a result of a difference in temperature. Thermal energy depends on the temperature difference between the bodies, the mass of the bodies, and the materials’ property.

Another important concept in relation to heat is that the temperature determines the direction of flow of thermal energy between two bodies in contact. The energy flows from the hotter object to the colder object. Two bodies in thermal contact will eventually reach the same temperature, which is called thermal equilibrium.

We looked at the 3 ways that heat is transferred: conduction, radiation, and convection.

Conduction is the heat transfer by particle collisions in a solid. For example, touching a hot stove is an example of conduction.

Radiation is a method of heat transfer that doesn’t require direct contact. Electromagnetic waves travel through space, and when these heat waves hit matter, they may be reflected, transmitted or absorbed. If they are absorbed, the particles in the matter may increase their movement and the overall temperature increases. An example of radiation is heat from the sun warming your face.

Convection is heat transfer in liquids and gases by a circulating path of fluid. Convection occurs when warmer areas of a liquid or gas rise to cooler areas in the liquid or gas. As this happens, cooler liquid or gas takes the place of the warmer areas. This cycle results in a continuous circulation pattern and heat is transferred to cooler areas. One example of this is when you boil water in a pot on the stove. The bubbles of water that rise are the hotter parts of the water rising to the cooler area of the water at the top of the pan.

These are the 3 ways that thermal energy is transferred: conduction, radiation, and convection.

One thing that really intrigued me is that energy always flows from the hotter object to the colder object. This is because the temperature of any object is related to the speed of the molecules. During collisions between molecules, the speeds tend to average out, increasing the speed of the slower molecules and decreasing the speed of the faster molecules so the kinetic energy and the related temperature tends to even out over time, causing the heat to transfer from “hot” to “cold”.
